01
Americans earn twice as much as Brits for the same work

We compared 70 occupations that exist in both countries — software developer, nurse, teacher, electrician, chef. On average, the American version of each job pays 2.1 times more in raw dollar-to-pound terms.

But that number is misleading without context. Here's why:

Wait — aren't dollars and pounds different?

Yes. There are three ways to compare pay across countries:

Nominal — just the raw numbers. A US developer earns $131K, a UK developer earns £55K. This is what job listings show, but it ignores that dollars and pounds have different values.

Exchange rate — convert everything to one currency. £1 is worth about $1.27. So that UK developer actually earns ~$70K. The gap shrinks from 2.1x to about 1.6x.

Purchasing power (PPP) — what can you actually buy with your salary? A flat in Manchester costs less than one in Austin. The NHS means no health insurance premiums. After adjusting for what money actually buys, the gap shrinks further to about 1.45x.

All three are "correct" — they just answer different questions. We show all three below.

That's 143 million American workers vs 33 million British — about 1 in 3 people in each country. Here's what that looks like:

03
The richer you are, the wider the gap

At the bottom of the pay scale (10th percentile), Americans earn 1.7x more. But at the top (90th percentile), it's 2.4x. The Atlantic pay gap isn't just about averages — it accelerates as you move up.

Think of it this way: a low-earning American worker gets an extra £16K per year compared to their British counterpart. A high-earning American gets an extra £73K. Same gap in principle, wildly different in impact.

What are percentiles?

If you lined up all occupations from lowest to highest paid, the 50th percentile (P50) is the one right in the middle — half earn more, half earn less. That's the median.

P10 means "only 10% of occupations pay less than this" — it's near the bottom. P90 means "90% earn less" — it's near the top. By comparing these, we can see whether the gap is consistent across the pay spectrum or whether it grows.

04
The lowest-paying US jobs earn more than the UK median

The worst-paid US occupations (around $31,000) would place above the midpoint in the UK (£34,250). UK bar staff earn just £9,166. Cleaners earn £11,852.

Why is UK low pay so extreme?

Two big reasons. First, UK data includes part-time workers in the median — so bar staff earning £9K likely work 15-20 hours a week, not full-time. US data reports full-time equivalent wages.

Second, the UK has a lower minimum wage floor (£11.44/hr vs ~$7.25-$15 in the US depending on state), though most UK workers earn above minimum.

The gap is real, but the raw numbers exaggerate it. If we compared hourly rates instead of annual totals, the picture would be less dramatic at the bottom.

05
The top earners in each country

US doctors earn $239,200. The highest-paid UK occupation — airline pilots — earns £107,712. The US has 14 occupations above $150K. The UK has zero above £110K.

Did you know?
UK train drivers earn £76,176 — making them one of the highest-paid occupations in Britain, despite requiring no university degree. In the US, locomotive engineers earn $74,610 — nearly identical. One of the rare cases of transatlantic pay parity.
06
Two countries, very different workforces

The US workforce is 4.3x larger (143 million vs 33 million). If the UK workforce was a single city, it would be roughly the size of Tokyo. The US workforce would be larger than the entire population of Japan.

But size aside, the composition tells a story about each society.

The story in the numbers
The UK's largest single occupation is care workers (911,000) — a direct reflection of the NHS and social care system. The US equivalent is hand laborers (6.9 million) — reflecting a manufacturing and logistics economy. What a country employs the most people to do reveals what it values.
07
Does a degree pay off?

In the US, a doctoral degree earns 2.9x what jobs requiring no formal credential pay ($135K vs $47K). The biggest jump is from high school to bachelor's — a 71% increase.

But here's the surprise: master's degrees barely out-earn bachelor's ($90K vs $93K). Unless you're going into medicine or law, the extra years may not pay off financially.

Why don't we have UK education-pay data?

The UK SOC classification describes entry requirements in text form ("a degree is typically required") but doesn't categorize them into structured levels the way the US BLS does. We could approximate this in a future update by parsing the text, but for now this section covers US data only.

08
Which jobs are growing — and which are vanishing?

The US Bureau of Labor Statistics projects employment changes through 2034. The fastest-growing jobs? Green energy and healthcare. The fastest-declining? Office admin and data entry.

The pattern
Jobs that require physical presence (wind turbine techs, nurse practitioners, solar installers) are booming. Jobs that involve processing information at a desk (tellers, desktop publishers, bill collectors) are contracting. This isn't just AI — it's a fundamental restructuring of what humans do for work.
